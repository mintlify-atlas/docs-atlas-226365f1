title: "Persona layers"
description: "The 5-layer model that encodes a colleague's personality — from inviolable hard rules down to interpersonal behavior patterns."
---

The Persona system encodes a person's character as a stack of five layers, each building on the one above. The layers are evaluated in priority order: Layer 0 always wins. No instruction, task, or correction can override it.

## Layer overview

| Layer | Name | What it encodes |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| **0** | Hard rules | Non-negotiable behavior rules derived directly from personality and culture tags. Highest priority. |
| **1** | Identity | Who they are: company, level, role, gender, MBTI, and how corporate culture has shaped them. |
| **2** | Expression style | How they communicate: vocabulary, sentence structure, emoji habits, formality level, and example dialogue. |
| **3** | Decision patterns | How they make decisions: what they prioritize, what triggers action, what they avoid, and how they say no. |
| **4** | Interpersonal behavior | How they interact with superiors, subordinates, peers, and how they behave under pressure. |

<Note>
  A sixth section — the **Correction log** — sits outside the numbered layers. It records every correction applied through conversation and takes precedence over the original layer content when a conflict exists.
</Note>

## Layer 0: Hard rules

Layer 0 is generated by translating personality tags and corporate culture tags into concrete, executable behavior rules. A rule must describe a specific situation and a specific action — never an adjective.

**What good Layer 0 rules look like:**

```
- When questioned about a decision, do not apologize or explain.
  Instead, ask "What's your basis for that judgment?"
- Before any meeting, say "Let me align on context first."
  If someone jumps to a solution without providing background, interrupt them.
- When evaluating any proposal, ask "What's the impact?" first.
  If they can't answer clearly, say "Figure that out before we discuss further."
- When assigned something you don't want to do, say
  "This is a great growth opportunity for you" and re-delegate it.
```

Rules that fail this standard ("you are blunt", "you value quality") are rejected during generation.

## Layer 1: Identity

Layer 1 records the colleague's structural identity: where they work, what level they hold, their role, gender, and MBTI type. It also captures how their corporate culture has shaped their behavior in concrete terms.

## Layer 2: Expression style

Layer 2 defines how the colleague talks. It covers:

- **Catchphrases and high-frequency words**: specific phrases they use repeatedly, quoted where possible from source material
- **Jargon**: company-internal terminology and when they deploy it
- **Sentence structure**: average length, tendency to bullet-point, whether conclusions come first
- **Emoji and punctuation**: none, occasional, or frequent — and which types
- **Formality level**: scored 1–5, with context-dependent variation (more formal upward, more casual in group chats)
- **Example dialogue**: verbatim-style examples for five scenarios — a basic question, a deadline check, a disagreement, a group @ mention, and a challenge to a past decision

The example dialogue section is the most important part of Layer 2. It must read like the person, not like a description of them.

## Layer 3: Decision patterns

Layer 3 captures how the colleague makes choices and navigates ambiguity:

- **Priority ordering**: what they optimize for first (efficiency, process compliance, data, relationships, resources, organizational politics)
- **Push triggers**: what causes them to proactively move something forward
- **Avoidance triggers**: what causes them to delay, deflect, or pretend not to notice
- **How they say no**: most people don't say no directly — this records the specific method (questions, redirection, silence, sub-delegation)
- **How they respond to being challenged**: explanation, counter-questioning, acknowledgment, deflection

## Layer 4: Interpersonal behavior

Layer 4 describes the colleague's relationship dynamics in four directions:

- **With superiors**: reporting frequency and style, how they claim credit, how they handle blame
- **With subordinates or junior colleagues**: how they assign work, their coaching willingness, their reaction when someone makes a mistake
- **With peers**: where they draw collaboration boundaries, how they handle disagreements in group chats, whether they're active or lurk until @-mentioned
- **Under pressure**: concrete behavioral changes when pushed on deadlines, questioned, or made to carry someone else's mistake

Each section includes 1–2 specific example scenarios, not general descriptions.

## How tags become rules

The `persona_analyzer` prompt handles tag translation automatically during Skill creation. The process:

1. User provides tags as free text (e.g. `INTJ blame-shifter ByteDance-style`)
2. Each tag is looked up in the translation table
3. The resulting behavior rules are written directly into Layer 0 of `persona.md`
4. Rules from multiple tags are combined — conflicts are flagged for user resolution

<Tip>
  Manual tags take priority over rules inferred from source material analysis. If the two conflict, the analyzer outputs both versions and asks the user to decide.
</Tip>
